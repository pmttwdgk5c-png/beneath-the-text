A project of questions, not conclusions
The sin of Sodom was sex. The sin of Sodom was pride, and contempt for the poor.

A palimpsest is a manuscript scraped clean and written over — where, under the right light, the erased text rises again beneath the new. This site is about that kind of erasure. What feels like the timeless surface of the Christian tradition is often a later hand, written over an older reading that didn't disappear so much as fall out of use — and in several cases, fall out of use for reasons that had more to do with empire, translation choices, and institutional needs than with what the text actually said.

This is not a site for people who have left the faith. It's a site for people still inside it, or adjacent to it, who have a nagging sense that the room they were raised in was smaller than the tradition it claimed. That nagging sense is correct. The faith was always wider, the sources are available, and the questions the sources raise are ones any honest reader is allowed to ask.

About the voice

Who is writing this

This project is written without a byline, and that's deliberate. The person behind it was formed inside the world it examines — homeschooled and raised in church, with formal training that included multiple years of New Testament Greek and graduate seminary education. The faith wasn't picked up at the edges; it was the center of the whole formation, and it crossed the breadth of American Protestantism: fundamentalist, conservative evangelical, non-denominational, Pentecostal and charismatic, Reformed, mainline, Anabaptist.

The questions raised here are not an outsider's. They are the questions of someone who has been a believer inside each of those rooms, and who is still, today, a practicing Christian — in the middle of a long, slow recalibration that is still in motion.

The name is withheld because attaching a person to an argument tends to do one of two things: it either discredits the ideas through the person, or it glorifies the person at the expense of the ideas. Both distract from what should be happening, which is your own work on what you're reading.
