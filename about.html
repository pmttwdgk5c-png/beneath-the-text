About the voice

This project is written without a byline, and that's deliberate.

The person behind it was formed inside the world it examines — homeschooled and raised in church, with formal training that included multiple years of New Testament Greek and the working tools to read the Hebrew with a lexicon — and beyond that, a graduate seminary education. The faith wasn't picked up at the edges; it was the center of the whole formation. And it wasn't a single stream of it, either. The formation crossed the breadth of American Protestantism: fundamentalist, conservative evangelical, non-denominational, Pentecostal and charismatic, Reformed, mainline Protestant, Anabaptist. The questions raised on this site are not an outsider's. They are the questions of someone who has been a believer inside each of those rooms, and who is still, today, a practicing Christian.

The training informed work in another field; it was never the route to a pulpit. What this site is, then, isn't the work of a pastor or a professor with an institutional position to defend. It's the work of a lay believer who did the graduate-level reading, who has spent years in a long, slow recalibration that is still in motion, and who has watched the rise of Christian nationalism with the particular pain of someone who recognizes the texts being misused and the readings being claimed as obvious. That recognition, and an ongoing personal reckoning with what the faith actually said before it was layered over, are the engine of what you're reading here.

Why no byline

The name is withheld for two reasons.

The first is principled: attaching a person to an argument tends to do one of two things — it either discredits the ideas through the person, or it glorifies the person at the expense of the ideas. Both distract from what should be happening, which is the reader's own work on what they're reading. This site is built to get out of that way.

The second is practical: a recalibration this slow is best done without public pressure to arrive somewhere. The work changes. The writer wants to be free to let it.

The standpoint

The standpoint is one of faith, not against it — a conviction that honest questions are owed honest sources, and that a two-thousand-year, worldwide faith can bear them. This site is not a deconstruction project in the sense that word now carries. It is a recovery project: the attempt to read the tradition at the depth it deserves, which turns out to be wider than the rooms where most of us first encountered it.

So this site is built to stand without the crutch of a byline. Every claim rests on a primary source you can open yourself, in an edition you can check. Don't trust the writer. Trust the documents — and your own reading of them.
